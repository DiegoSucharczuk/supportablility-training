ü§îWhat is Supportability?

Supportability is the quality of our interactions with customers when they need technical help.

It's about:

  • How clearly we communicate
  • How well we understand their problems
  • How effectively we guide them to solutions
  • How professionally we represent our company

"Good support isn't just about fixing issues, it's about making customers feel heard, understood, and confident."

Technical support is difficult because we need to balance many things at once:

  • Technical accuracy - Getting the facts right
  • Clear communication - Making complex topics understandable
  • Professional tone - Being respectful and confident
  • Time pressure - Responding quickly without sacrificing quality
  • Customer emotions - Dealing with frustrated or confused customers
  • Incomplete information - Often missing critical details

üö´Common Pitfalls to Avoid

‚ùå Being Vague

"The system might be having issues. Try restarting."

Why it's wrong: No specific diagnosis, unclear what to restart, customer doesn't learn anything.

‚ùå Technical Jargon Without Context

"Check your TLS handshake and verify the cipher suite compatibility."

Why it's wrong: Assumes customer understands terms, no guidance on how to check, no explanation.

‚ùå Deflecting Responsibility

"This looks like a problem with your network team."

Why it's wrong: Passes blame, doesn't offer to help coordinate, customer feels abandoned.

‚ùå No Follow-up Plan

"Let me know if that works." (Then never checks back)

Why it's wrong: Puts burden on customer, no timeline, no proactive engagement.

Remember: Even if something isn't our fault, it's still our responsibility to help the customer find a solution.

‚ùìThe Customer Problem

Support Engineer wrote to R&D:

My customer has opened a case regarding their Linux providers Unix servers (CP v14.00, CLIPasswordsdk).

The Issue:
After a recent OS Patch, the customers Linux Providers have started to fail Allowed Machine Authentication.

The Providers were whitelisted in the Application's authentication list with their short hostname.

After the patch, the customer needs to add the IP/FQDN under allowed machines. The problem: they have 100's of applications.

They found that the patch removed an entry from the host file.

Customer's Question:
Is aimagent depending on /etc/hostfile for shortname? If not, what could be the reason for sudden break?

[sysabc @SGDPSR00002][PROD]: hostname -f
SGDP.int.bar.com

[sysabc @SGDPSR00002][PROD]: hostname -s
SGDPSR00002

Support Engineer asks: How does the CP determine the hostname? This would help the customer plan how to fix this without mass updates.

Best regards,
Manuel Ortega

‚ùåWhat R&D Actually Wrote

Hi Daniel

The FQDN apparently changed from the Linux upgrade

In Linux, the FQDN is determined from the "hostname -f" command.

What Diego talks about with the api call is relevant for Windows.

CP blocks every communication and password request from unknown hostname, hence if host name was changed it will block all of the requests (from this unauthorized end point)

The client should update his application's authentication list with the new hostnames

What's Wrong:

  • ‚ùå No empathy or acknowledgment of customer impact
  • ‚ùå Dismissive tone ("apparently changed")
  • ‚ùå Blames customer ("The client should...")
  • ‚ùå No action plan or timeline
  • ‚ùå No offer to help or guide
  • ‚ùå No clear next steps
  • ‚ùå No follow-up commitment
  • ‚ùå Missing internal note to support

‚úÖHow R&D Should Write

Hi Daniel,
I understand that the customer reports the application stopped receiving passwords from the CP after the Linux upgrade. Based on the behavior and the logs they shared, the CP now treats the server as an unrecognized source. The upgrade introduced a new FQDN.

Next steps

‚Ä¢ I will guide you through each step

**Internal note to support: Please guide the customer in each step**
